§ 01 — Orientation

Overview

Think of this section as your "day one" primer. By the end, you will understand what this product does, who uses it, and what the key terms mean — so when a customer calls, you can follow along immediately.

What Engage Digital is

The one-sentence version: Engage Digital (ED) is a tool that lets a company's customer service team manage all of their digital conversations — email, text messages, Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, live chat, and more — from one single screen instead of jumping between ten different apps.

Think of it like this: Imagine you work at a help desk and customers can reach you through email, text, social media, and your website chat — all at once. Without ED, you would need to have Gmail open, Facebook open, Instagram open, WhatsApp open, and a chat tool open — all at the same time — and somehow keep track of who said what and where. ED replaces all of that with one unified inbox. A customer can email you on Monday, then message you on Facebook on Tuesday, and the agent sees both messages in the same conversation view.

The product organizes customer communication into five main categories:

  • Social media — Facebook posts and comments, Instagram DMs, X (Twitter) messages, WhatsApp, even LinkedIn
  • Live Chat — a chat widget that lives on the customer's website (the little "Chat with us" bubble you see on many sites)
  • Mobile messaging — in-app chat using a software kit that developers embed inside mobile apps
  • Email — inbound customer emails and outbound replies, handled entirely within ED
  • Communities — online forums and customer support communities
Why this matters for support When a customer calls you and says "our RingCentral email isn't working," they almost always mean their email channel inside Engage Digital — not RingCentral's hosted email product. These are completely different systems. Learning to spot this distinction will save you from misrouting tickets on day one.

ED was originally built by a company called Dimelo, based in Paris, France. RingCentral acquired Dimelo in 2019 and rebranded it. This is important because you will still see "Dimelo" all over the place — in URLs, in documentation, in log files, and in how the Paris-based engineering team talks about the product. "Dimelo" and "Engage Digital" are the same thing.

ED now sits alongside RingCX (the voice/phone contact center) in RingCentral's product family. Many customers use both — they handle phone calls in RingCX and handle emails, chats, and social media in Engage Digital. You may also see cases labeled "RingCX Digital" — this is the same Engage Digital platform, just rebranded for customers who buy it as part of the RingCX bundle.

Key terms you need to know

Every product has its own vocabulary. Here are the terms you will hear on every call and see in every ticket. Take five minutes to read through these — they are the building blocks of everything else in this guide.

Channel (or "Source")
A single connection to one communication method. For example: one email address, one Facebook page, or one WhatsApp phone number. If a customer has three email addresses for support, that is three separate channels in ED. You may also hear this called a "content source."
Message (or "Content")
A single message — one email, one Facebook comment, one text message. It is the smallest unit of communication in ED. Every message has a unique ID that looks like a long string of letters and numbers.
Thread
A conversation — a group of related messages. Think of a thread like an email chain: the original message and all its replies are grouped together. When customers report that "replies are going to the wrong place," they are describing a threading problem.
Task (or "Intervention")
A piece of work assigned to an agent. When a new customer message arrives and is ready for an agent to handle, it becomes a task. One thread (conversation) can generate multiple tasks over its lifetime — the first response, a reopening, a transfer to another team, etc.
Identity
The customer profile — the person on the other side of the conversation. ED builds a profile for each customer that can link together their email address, phone number, social media accounts, and other info. If a customer contacts you via email and later via Facebook, ED can recognize it is the same person.
Agent
A customer service representative — the person using ED to respond to customers. Each agent has a "role" that controls what they can see and do. Roles are like permission levels (for example, an Agent role might be more limited than a Supervisor or Admin role).
Channel Group (Queue)
A waiting line for tasks. Think of it like the lines at a bank — each channel group is a separate line, and agents are assigned to serve specific lines. This is how ED decides which agent gets which task. If no agents are available, the task waits in the queue.
Category (or "Disposition")
A label that gets applied to a conversation — like tagging an email in Gmail. Categories are used for reporting ("How many billing complaints did we get this month?") and routing ("Send all billing complaints to Team A"). Some customers require their agents to select a category before they can finish handling a task — this is called a "mandatory disposition."
Rules Engine
The automation system — "if this, then that" for incoming messages. For example: "If a message contains the word 'refund,' automatically categorize it as Billing and send it to the Billing queue." Rules run automatically when messages arrive.
Supervision
The live monitoring dashboard. Team leaders use this to see, in real time, how many tasks are waiting, which agents are available, and how the workload is distributed. Think of it like a command center view.
Customer-to-ED translator Here is how to translate common customer language into ED terms. When a customer says "our inbox" they usually mean a Channel Group or Folder. "Our Facebook page" is a Channel. "The conversation" is a Thread. "The ticket" or "the case" is a Task. "The customer record" is an Identity. "The auto-sort rules" are the Rules Engine.

How to recognize ED URLs in the wild

Every customer has their own private instance of ED, and you can tell which customer it belongs to just by looking at the web address. There are two URL formats you will see every day — they both point to the same product, just with different branding:

What you will seeExampleWhat it means
Dimelo format (older) orange.engagement.dimelo.com The original domain from before the RingCentral rebrand. Most European customers still use this. The customer name is the first part of the URL ("orange" in this example).
RingCentral format (newer) frontier.digital.ringcentral.com The rebranded domain. Newer customers and North American accounts typically use this. Same product, different web address.
Admin pages .../admin/content_sources/.../edit If you see /admin/ in a URL a customer shares, you are looking at a configuration page. The word after /admin/ tells you what they are configuring (channels, rules, agents, etc.).
Conversation links .../content_threads/69ba9d3a... A direct link to a specific conversation. The long string of characters at the end is the conversation's unique ID — save this if you need to escalate a case.
Quick rule of thumb If a URL contains dimelo.com or digital.ringcentral.com, you are looking at Engage Digital. The customer name is always the first part of the address, before the first period. Write it down — you will need it when logging the case.

§ 02 — Understanding the agent experience

What Agents See & Do

To help customers, you need to understand what their agents experience. This section walks you through the product from the agent's perspective — what they see when they log in, how they handle customer messages, and what goes wrong.

What agents see when they log in

When an agent logs into Engage Digital, they land on their main workspace. Here is what matters:

  • The status indicator (top of screen) — This shows whether the agent is "Available," "Away," or "Offline." This is critical because the agent's status controls whether they receive work. If they are set to "Away," the system will not send them new tasks. If they switch from "Available" to "Away" while they have a task, that task may be taken away from them and sent back to the queue.
  • The left sidebar — This is how agents switch between different views of their work. There are four views, but three of them are the main ones agents use every day (described below).
  • The conversation panel — When an agent opens a task, they see the full conversation history with the customer — every message, from every channel, in order. They type their reply at the bottom.
What customers often call these Customers rarely use the official names. They will say "my agents can't see any messages" (they might be in the wrong view), "the screen is empty" (the agent might be set to Away), or "tasks disappeared" (they probably timed out). Understanding the interface helps you translate these complaints.

The three ways agents receive work

ED has three different ways to organize how agents get their tasks. Most customers use one of these as their primary mode, and different modes behave differently — which matters when troubleshooting.

ModeHow it works (plain English)When tasks disappearWho uses this
Routing Mode Tasks are pushed to agents automatically. The agent does not choose what to work on — the system decides. Think of it like a call center where the next call rings on your phone automatically. Yes — if the agent does not respond within a set time limit (usually 15 minutes, but configurable), the task is taken back and given to someone else. High-volume teams (e.g., telecom companies handling hundreds of messages a day)
Inboxes Mode Agents pull their own work. They browse a shared inbox and click on the conversations they want to handle. Think of it like a shared email inbox where you pick which email to answer. No automatic timeout — once an agent claims a task, it stays with them until they finish it or manually hand it off. Smaller teams, B2B support, case-management style work
Folders Mode Messages are organized into folders based on filters or categories. Agents browse folders and pick what to work on. Think of it like email folders — "Billing," "Technical Support," "VIP Customers," etc. No timeout. Backlog cleanup, supervisor triage, or secondary view alongside Routing
Common troubleshooting scenario Customer says: "My agent's task disappeared after 34 minutes, but our timeout is set to 15 minutes."
What to check: (1) Which channel group (queue) was the task assigned to? Each queue has its own timeout setting. (2) Did the agent change their status to "Away"? That can release the task immediately, regardless of the timer. (3) Was there a rule that transferred the task to a different queue with a different timeout?

How an agent handles a task, step by step

This is the basic workflow that every agent follows. You should be able to walk a customer through this from memory:

  1. Agent sets their status to "Available." Nothing happens until they do this — the system will not send them work while they are Away or Offline.
  2. A task appears. If in Routing Mode, it pops up automatically. If in Inboxes or Folders, the agent clicks on a conversation to open it.
  3. Agent reads the conversation history. They can see every message the customer has sent, any previous replies from other agents, and information about the customer (their Identity profile).
  4. Agent writes a reply. They can also attach files, use saved response templates, and add their email signature.
  5. Agent applies a category (if required). Some customers configure "mandatory dispositions" — this means the agent MUST select a label (like "Billing Question" or "Technical Issue") before they can close the task. If an agent says they "can't complete" a task, check whether mandatory categories are turned on.
  6. Agent finishes. They can: Send & Complete (reply and close), Ignore (dismiss without replying — for spam or duplicates), or Transfer (hand it to another agent or team).
Your best friend: Impersonation ED has a built-in feature that lets admins (and support staff with admin access) "impersonate" any agent — meaning you can log in as that agent and see exactly what they see, without needing their password. This is the fastest way to diagnose "I can't see X" or "Y isn't working for me" complaints. Look for the impersonate option in the admin panel under the agent's profile. It leaves an audit trail, so it is fully sanctioned.

When agents send messages first (outbound)

Agents can start new conversations — not just reply to incoming ones. This is most common with email and SMS. When they do:

  • If the recipient is already in the system (their email or phone number matches an existing customer profile), the new message gets linked to that customer automatically.
  • If the recipient is brand new, ED creates a new customer profile (Identity) for them automatically.

If a customer reports that outbound messages "aren't creating a customer record," collect the specific conversation links and the channel name, and escalate — this is a known edge case that requires investigation by Tier 3.

The Supervision dashboard (for team leads)

Team leaders and supervisors have a special real-time dashboard that shows them the state of their operation at a glance: how many tasks are waiting, who is online, and how busy each agent is.

Important: The Supervision view has two tabs — one that shows numbers by queue and one that shows numbers by agent. These two views will often show different numbers for what seems like the same thing. This is by design, not a bug — they count differently. This confuses customers so often that it is one of the Top 10 Issues in this guide (see Issue #10 for the full explanation and what to tell the customer).

§ 03 — Navigating the admin panel

Admin Portal Guide

When a customer needs help configuring their system, they (or you) will be working in the Admin panel. This section explains what each part of the admin panel does, and what typically goes wrong in each area.

Channels — where messages come from

A "Channel" (also called a "Content Source" in the admin panel) is a connection to one communication method. Setting up and troubleshooting channels is a huge part of support work. Here is what you need to know about each type:

Email channels

Email is the most common channel type and the source of the most support tickets (33% of all Tier 3 cases). There are two ways email works in ED:

  • Postmark (the default for most customers) — Customer emails are received by a service called Postmark, which then forwards them into ED. When agents reply, the reply also goes out through Postmark. If an email fails to send, the problem is almost always on the Postmark side, not inside ED. The most common issue: an email address gets added to Postmark's "blocked" list (called the "suppression list") because a previous email bounced or was marked as spam. See Issue #1 for the full troubleshooting playbook.
  • IMAP/SMTP (for customers who use their own mail server) — ED connects directly to the customer's email server (like Microsoft Exchange). This is slower because ED has to check the server periodically for new messages instead of receiving them instantly. Some customers also run email through security appliances like Fortimail that scan attachments.
The #1 email question you will get "Why can't we send emails to [specific address]?" — The answer is almost always: that address is on Postmark's suppression list. You fix it in Postmark (not in ED). See Issue #1.

SMS channels

SMS works through phone carriers — Bandwidth (for North American numbers) or Twilio (for other regions). Each SMS channel has a phone number associated with it. When SMS breaks, it is usually because:

  • The phone number was changed or re-configured at the carrier, and ED's connection is outdated.
  • The phone number does not have the right permissions to send text messages at the carrier level.
  • The phone number has been flagged as spam by the carrier — ED cannot fix this; it requires working with the carrier.

If an SMS channel shows an error, coordinate with our internal Telco Ops team — they manage the carrier relationships.

Live Chat channels (Messaging SDK)

This is the chat widget — the "Chat with us" bubble that appears on a customer's website. The customer sets it up by adding a small piece of code (a "snippet") to their website. Common issues:

  • "The chat bubble isn't showing up" — Usually a problem with how the code snippet was added to their website, or their website's security settings are blocking it.
  • "The pre-chat form stopped working" — Someone changed the form settings in the admin panel. Check the channel configuration.
  • "Chat history disappeared after we changed a setting" — If the customer switched their chat from "logged-in users only" mode to "open to everyone" mode, conversation history does not carry over. This is a known limitation.

Social media channels (Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, X/Twitter)

Social channels connect to the customer's social media accounts. They all require the customer to authorize ED to access their social media pages (similar to when you "Connect with Facebook" on a website). This authorization can break for several reasons:

  • Someone changed the password on the social media account.
  • The social media platform (Meta, X, etc.) revoked access due to a policy change.
  • The authorization simply expired (they do not last forever).

When a social channel stops working, the first step is almost always: ask the customer to re-authorize the connection from the channel's settings page in the admin panel. For X/Twitter specifically: direct messages started arriving encrypted in early 2026, which requires a special PIN to be configured. If an X channel suddenly stops importing DMs, ask if they have set up the PIN.

Google channels (Google Play, Google Business Messages)

These connect to Google's systems and occasionally break when Google updates their service. Errors can persist for weeks if they are on Google's side. If a Google channel shows "Invalid request" errors consistently, escalate to Tier 3 — it may require engineering involvement.

Agents & Roles — the most common admin headache

Managing agents and their permissions is straightforward, but there is one rule that catches everyone off guard and generates a significant number of support tickets. Understanding this rule now will save you hours of troubleshooting later.

The permission rule (read this carefully)

In ED, a manager can only edit people whose permissions are equal to or less than their own.

Analogy: Imagine a company where a Director can manage Managers, and Managers can manage Agents. Now imagine someone gives the Agent role a special permission that the Manager role does not have (like access to a new feature). Suddenly, Managers can no longer edit Agents — because the system says "you can't manage someone with a permission you don't have." All the edit fields turn gray, and nothing in the interface explains why.

What the customer will say: "Our Director and Manager roles suddenly can't edit anyone. Nothing changed."

What actually happened: Someone added a permission to a lower-level role, and that permission is not present on the Director/Manager role. The fix: add the same permission to the Director and Manager roles so they are equal or higher.

How to diagnose it:

  1. Use the "impersonate" feature to log in as the affected manager.
  2. Try to edit a specific agent — confirm that the fields are grayed out.
  3. Open the manager's role settings in one browser tab, and the agent's role settings in another.
  4. Compare the permission checkboxes. Any permission that is checked on the agent's role but NOT checked on the manager's role is the culprit.
  5. Add the missing permission(s) to the manager's role. The edit functionality will immediately start working again.

Rules Engine — the "if this, then that" system

The Rules Engine is how customers automate message handling. It is like setting up email filters in Gmail, but more powerful. Each rule has three parts:

  1. Trigger — When does the rule run? (When a new message arrives? When a conversation is updated? When a new customer is created?)
  2. Conditions — What does the rule look for? (Does the message contain certain words? Is it from a specific channel? Is the customer tagged as VIP?)
  3. Actions — What does the rule do? (Apply a category, send it to a specific queue, set the priority, auto-ignore it.)

When a customer says "my rule isn't working": Check the rule's event log (visible in the admin panel under the rule's history). The system records every time a rule runs and what it did. Sometimes the rule ran but applied to the wrong object — for example, it categorized the individual message instead of the whole conversation. The event log will show you exactly what happened.

About mandatory categories (dispositions): Customers can require agents to pick a category before closing a task. This is called a "mandatory disposition." A recent update (April 2026) changed how this works with transferred tasks — agents can no longer skip categorization when using the "bypass transfer" option. If customers report that agents are "being forced to categorize when they didn't have to before," this is the reason.

Routing & Channel Groups — how work gets distributed

A Channel Group is a queue — it is the system that decides which agents get which tasks. Here are the four settings that matter most:

  • Interaction timeout — How long a task sits with an agent before the system takes it back and gives it to someone else. Think of it like: "If you don't answer within 15 minutes, the call goes to the next available person."
  • Agents — Which agents are assigned to this queue. If an agent is not in the queue's member list, they will never receive tasks from it.
  • Channels — Which channels feed into this queue. For example, "All Facebook messages go to Queue A, all emails go to Queue B."
  • Priority — If an agent is a member of multiple queues, which queue's tasks should they get first? The queue with the highest priority wins.
Routing is the #2 support issue Routing and queue configuration is the second most common reason customers call support (28% of T3 cases). If an agent says "I'm not getting any tasks," the first things to check are: (1) Is the agent set to Available? (2) Is the agent a member of the right channel group? (3) Is the channel group connected to the right channel? These three checks resolve most routing issues.

Identities — customer profiles

An "Identity" is ED's name for a customer profile. When a customer contacts the company through any channel, ED creates a record for them. Admins can:

  • View an identity to see all conversations that customer has had across all channels.
  • Merge two identities if the same customer was accidentally created twice (e.g., they emailed from two different addresses).
  • Anonymize an identity to comply with GDPR/privacy regulations — this removes personal information while keeping the conversation statistics intact for reporting.
  • Block an identity on specific channels to prevent them from contacting the company.

API access (for technical customers)

Some customers use ED's API (Application Programming Interface) to connect ED with their own systems — for example, automatically pulling conversation data into their CRM, or programmatically creating categories. You do not need to understand how APIs work, but you should know:

  • API access is controlled by tokens (like passwords) that are generated in the admin panel under Advanced Settings → API Access Tokens.
  • Each token is tied to a specific user account and inherits that user's permissions.
  • If a customer says "our API stopped working," the first thing to check is whether the token was deactivated (this can happen if the user account it is attached to was modified).
  • Not everything in the admin panel is available through the API. Customers frequently ask for API access to create channels or manage the Rules Engine — these are not currently available, and this is a known limitation.
§ 04 — For Tier 3

Backend, Logs & Escalation

The architecture, just enough of it. You do not need to be able to ship code. You do need to know where the moving parts live, what they are called, and how to pull logs without bothering engineering.

Topology at a glance

ED is a Ruby on Rails monolith fronted by a web tier, backed by MongoDB, with a fleet of worker processes consuming a job queue. It is multi-tenant, sharded, and runs multi-region.

Bugsnag

app.bugsnag.com — aggregates exceptions. Useful for spotting error spikes and finding the stacktrace of a known error class. Always cross-reference a Bugsnag exception count against the tenant's ticket volume before escalating a one-off as a systemic issue.

Source code (GitLab)

git.ringcentral.com/engage-digital/engage-digital — you do not need to be able to write code, but being able to read the relevant file when a ticket references one will massively speed up engineering handoffs. The paths you will see most often:

  • lib/realtime/{channel}.rb — realtime inbound handlers per channel type.
  • app/models/{channel}/synchronizer.rb — the thread-matching / import logic per channel.
  • app/models/{channel}/timeline_item.rb — representation of a message/content in that channel.

Postmark dashboard

For every email-delivery ticket, Postmark is the authoritative source for whether the email left ED, whether it was accepted, and whether the destination address is on the suppression list. Bookmark:

  • Inbound stream — shows every inbound webhook ED received from Postmark.
  • Suppression list — hard bounces, spam complaints, manual unsubscribes.
  • Message search — by subject, recipient, or Message-ID header.

MongoDB lookups for Tier 3

You will rarely query Mongo directly, but you will read log lines that show queries. Decoding them is straightforward. The common shape:

MONGODB | ringcentral.web.mongo-01:27017 |
  engagement_console_production_s2.find |
  STARTED |
  { "find" => "fizz#content_threads",
    "filter" => { "deleted_at" => nil,
                  "foreign_id" => "b4b47031-461f-4e16-8ac9-b0f3099d231d",
                  "_type" => "MobileMessaging::ContentThread" },
    "hint" => { "foreign_id" => 1 },
    "limit" => 1 }

What this tells you: the RealtimeWorker, running on the fizz tenant (shard 2), is searching for a thread by foreign_id. If no match, a new thread gets created — which is how thread-splitting bugs manifest. If you find this query in the logs and it returns zero results, but the thread should exist, that is your smoking gun.

Tier 3 heuristic When a customer reports "messages are going to a new thread instead of the existing one," the fastest Tier 3 path is: (1) get the thread URL and the new-thread URL, (2) extract both Mongo IDs, (3) in Kibana, search content_id:"X" OR content_id:"Y" to find the RealtimeWorker logs, (4) compare the foreign_id each worker was looking up. The divergence between them is the root cause. This is true for email (Message-ID header mismatch), WhatsApp (UUID mismatch), and mobile messaging alike.

Escalation path

The canonical escalation ladder, with what each tier should have tried before handing off:

  1. Tier 1 (L1) — in-product replication, customer-side config check, KB lookup. Owns first-touch.
  2. Tier 2 (L2) — impersonate user, admin portal investigation, Postmark / Bandwidth dashboard checks, SFDC case linking.
  3. Tier 3 (L3) — Kibana logs, Bugsnag, Mongo query inspection, source-code reading. Reproduces with test tenant where possible.
  4. Paris Engineering — code-level diagnosis, Jira ticket filing (jira.ringcentral.com), hotfix coordination. Ticket prefixes: RD-* (core), EDEQ-* (messaging), INIT-* (UX), DMO-* (legacy).

Email delivery & synchronization 142 T3 cases (33%) · ~95 chat msgs

Symptoms

  • "Synchronization failed — The following emails are inactive or marked previous messages as spam"
  • Outbound emails silently fail for specific recipients.
  • Email delivery delays (30+ minutes) for entire tenant.
  • Emails not arriving in order of receipt.
  • IMAP/SMTP channels losing sync with customer mail server.

Root cause

For Postmark tenants: addresses on the suppression list (hard bounce, spam complaint). The "reactivate" inside ED does nothing — it must be cleared on the Postmark side. For IMAP tenants: sync folder removed or mail server connection dropped. For delivery delays: Postmark webhook latency between receiving the email and posting to ED.

Resolution playbook

  1. Check account.postmarkapp.com → server → Suppressions tab. Search for the affected address.
  2. If found, review the reason (HardBounce, SpamComplaint). Clear if customer confirms recipient is valid.
  3. For delays: check status.postmarkapp.com for incidents. Pull Kibana webhook receipt timestamps.
  4. For IMAP: check the channel admin page for "last sync time." Verify IMAP credentials with customer's IT.
  5. For "emails not in order": this is a known limitation of webhook-based ingestion — timestamps are Postmark-receipt, not original-send.

Routing & queue configuration 122 T3 cases (28%)

Symptoms

  • Digital tasks not reaching available agents despite queue being active.
  • Tasks stuck in "pre-queued" state indefinitely.
  • Queue events not firing in sequence (e.g., "Send Message" step skipped).
  • Manual assignment from Inboxes showing incomplete agent list.
  • Priority routing not prioritizing correctly across channel groups.
  • "Follow the Sun" routing failing when time-zone handoffs trigger.
  • Emails actioned from review inbox not removed from main queue.
  • Transferred emails not appearing in the target inbox.

Root cause

Routing failures have many roots: (1) agent not a member of the correct channel group, (2) agent presence set to Away or Offline, (3) concurrent interaction limit reached on the agent, (4) queue event ordering misconfigured (events fire sequentially — if step 1 fails, step 2 never starts), (5) channel group filter excluding the source, (6) interaction timeout expiring before agent action.

Resolution playbook

  1. Confirm the agent's channel group membership and their current presence status.
  2. Check the queue event sequence in Admin → Channel Groups → edit → Events. Walk through each step.
  3. For "pre-queued" states: check whether any prerequisite event (authentication, bot handoff) is blocking advancement.
  4. For Follow-the-Sun: verify business hours configuration on each channel group involved in the handoff.
  5. For priority routing: the priority is set per-channel-group. If an agent belongs to multiple groups, the highest-priority group wins. Verify the numeric priority values.
  6. When tasks are stuck: check if mandatory dispositions are enabled — if an agent ignores a task without categorizing, it may recirculate rather than complete.

Typical customers

The Hut.com, Acquire BPO, Direct Travel, Arrowhead Holdings, MedOne, Adelaide Crows, Rotherham, Now Prepay, Collaborative Neuroscience.

Analytics & reporting discrepancies 47 T3 cases (11%) · ~22 chat msgs

Symptoms

  • Report shows no data for a time period that should have data.
  • Analytics dashboard loading extremely slowly or returning errors.
  • Real-time dashboard lagging 20–30 seconds behind actual state.
  • Conversation volume totals differ between standard analytics and GoodData/Advanced Analytics.
  • Timezone mismatches between date-range filter and actual data.
  • Exported analytics CSV containing duplicate thread IDs.

Root cause

  • Missing data — the StatsDenormWorker that denormalizes stats is backlogged or encountered errors. This is the same worker that causes supervision discrepancies (Issue #10).
  • Slow loading — large tenants with high message volumes hit MongoDB query timeouts when the date range is too wide or the category filter is too broad.
  • Real-time lag — the real-time dashboard polls on an interval; 20–30 second delays are within expected range for large-scale tenants.
  • GoodData discrepancies — GoodData has its own ETL pipeline with a separate sync schedule. Data may be 24–48 hours behind the native analytics.

Resolution playbook

  1. For missing data: narrow the date range. Try single-day slices. If some days have data and others don't, the issue is StatsDenormWorker — escalate to T3 with the exact missing date range.
  2. For slow loading: reduce the scope — fewer channels, shorter date range, simpler report type. Large tenants (Asiacell, Orange) regularly hit this.
  3. For timezone issues: confirm the tenant's timezone setting in Admin → Settings. Reports use this timezone, not the browser's local time.
  4. For GoodData: confirm the ETL sync schedule with engineering. GoodData issues are often transient and resolve on the next sync cycle.

Typical customers

Asiacell, Henner Medical, Zain Jordan, SCC France, The Hut.com, RC Internal, Frontier Airlines.

Chat & Messaging widget issues 46 T3 cases (11%) · ~18 chat msgs

Symptoms

  • Chat bubble not appearing on customer's website.
  • Pre-chat form stops collecting data — agents receive chats with no customer info.
  • Chat distributed to an agent who appears disconnected.
  • "Extra messages" being sent by bot channel at session start.
  • Conversation history lost after migrating from authenticated-only to open-to-all-visitors mode.
  • Chat session not being handed over from bot to live agent.
  • Queue position not displaying to the waiting visitor.

Root cause

  • Bubble not appearing — the JavaScript snippet is loaded but the customer's CSP (Content Security Policy) headers are blocking the ED domain. Or the snippet is placed inside a conditional block that isn't executing.
  • Pre-chat form failure — this is typically a recent config change that broke the form fields. Check the channel admin page → pre-chat settings.
  • Agent appears disconnected — race condition between presence update and task assignment. The agent's session may have timed out on the browser side while the server still considered them available.
  • Auth-to-open migration — conversation history does not persist across this mode change. This is a known limitation (EDEQ-518).

Resolution playbook

  1. For bubble issues: open the customer's site in Chrome DevTools → Console. Look for CSP violations or JavaScript errors referencing the ED domain.
  2. For pre-chat forms: impersonate a visitor by loading the chat snippet on a test page (do NOT use W3Schools — pre-chat config does not render in isolated sandboxes).
  3. For bot handoff failures: check the Engage Messaging channel config → bot settings → handoff trigger. Verify the bot is correctly signaling the handover event.
  4. For queue position display: this is a feature availability question — confirm with engineering whether the customer's plan includes queue position messaging.

Typical customers

Outremer Telecom, BMCE Maroc, RED By SFR, Odigo, Ziggo, Zain Jordan, MedOne, Videotron/Fizz.

7 Complex diagnosis — Tier 3 specialty

Thread aggregation failures 35 T3 cases · ~80 chat msgs

Symptoms

  • Customer reply creates a new thread instead of joining the existing conversation.
  • Two (or more) threads exist for what should be one conversation.
  • Affects email, WhatsApp, and Mobile Messaging channels.

Root cause

Thread matching is done by foreign_id lookup in MongoDB. When the inbound message's foreign_id doesn't match any existing thread, a new one is created. Common causes: Amazon SES Message-ID differs from ED-stored ID; email client strips In-Reply-To headers; WhatsApp UUID lookup races the outbound write; late imports cause duplicate thread creation.

Resolution playbook

  1. Collect both thread URLs. Extract the 24-hex Mongo IDs.
  2. In Kibana: thread_id:"X" OR thread_id:"Y". Find the RealtimeWorker logs.
  3. Compare the foreign_id each worker was looking up.
  4. For email: pull the raw message from Postmark, check References: and In-Reply-To: headers vs. the outbound Message-ID:.
  5. Report all four values (both thread IDs, both foreign_ids) to engineering.

Typical customers

Henner, Telefónica Germany, Fizz/Videotron, Orange SA, ENGIE, La Banque Postale, Advanced Water.

API, SDK & Webhook errors 32 T3 cases (7.5%)

Symptoms

  • REST API returning 500 errors on previously working endpoints.
  • API PUT/PATCH succeeds (returns 200) but the field is not actually updated (e.g., cc_blacklist).
  • Webhook notifications missing the deferredAt field for deferred tasks.
  • App SDK extensions not executing in the agent workspace.
  • Mobile SDK: date fields not displaying on Android.
  • API access token spontaneously deactivated.
  • Undeliverable emails not returning errors via API.

Root cause

Many API fields are silently read-only — they accept updates without error but don't persist the change (the cc_blacklist issue from the chat data is a prime example). Webhook payloads can be incomplete when a field's value is null at delivery time. App SDK failures are usually CSP or CORS issues in the customer's browser environment. Token deactivation can occur when the owning user account is modified.

Resolution playbook

  1. For 500 errors: check Bugsnag for the exception class. Pull the request logs in Kibana with the API endpoint path.
  2. For silent no-op updates: verify the field is listed as writable in the API docs (developers.ringcentral.com/engage/digital/api-reference). If it's documented as writable but doesn't persist, this is a bug — file a Jira under RD-*.
  3. For webhook issues: check the webhook config in Admin → Advanced Settings → Webhooks. Verify the endpoint is returning 200. Check the retry policy.
  4. For SDK rendering: check browser console for CORS/CSP errors. The SDK domains must be allowlisted in the customer's CSP headers.
  5. For token deactivation: check the audit log for changes to the user who owns the token. Regenerate from Admin → API Access Tokens.

Typical customers

Odigo (heavy API user), SNCF, ORANGE BUSINESS SERVICES, Ooredoo, Ziggo, RED By SFR, Nine Communications.

9 New to ranking — data operations

Data export failures 22 T3 cases · ~17 chat mentions

Symptoms

  • Scheduled automated export did not run at the expected time.
  • Export file is corrupted or truncated — cannot be opened.
  • Raw data export is delayed by hours or missing entirely.
  • Duplicate thread IDs appearing in satisfaction survey export.
  • "Export requires sign in" — export URL requires re-authentication.
  • Graph cannot be exported as image — button fails silently.

Root cause

Automated exports are run by a background worker. When the worker fails (timeout, memory pressure, or MongoDB query overload), the export silently doesn't run — there is no retry, and no notification to the customer. Corrupted files usually mean the export was interrupted mid-write. Raw data delays are tied to the same StatsDenormWorker pipeline as analytics issues.

Resolution playbook

  1. For missed automated exports: check the worker log in Kibana for the tenant and the expected export time. Look for cnv01 or the shard-specific worker log.
  2. For corrupted files: have the customer re-trigger the export manually. If it fails again, narrow the date range — large exports are more likely to timeout.
  3. For raw data delays: check StatsDenormWorker backlog status. If delayed by more than a few hours, escalate to engineering.
  4. For "requires sign in": this is a session-token issue. The export URL contains a session token that expires. Customer should re-navigate to the export from within the admin UI.

Typical customers

RC Internal, Ziggo, RED By SFR, ENGIE, SCC France, The Office Gurus, Mike's Carwash.

10 Persistent confusion

Supervision metric discrepancies 26 T3 cases · ~25 chat msgs

Symptoms

  • Agents view shows 217 pending but Channel Groups shows 0.
  • "Pending" count doesn't decrease after agents complete interactions.
  • Dashboard metrics (active time, handle time) exceed interval duration — e.g., 2100 seconds in a 1800-second interval.

Root cause

The two views measure different things: Channel Groups counts unassigned queue tasks; Agents counts tasks held by specific agents. A high Agents count + low Channel Groups count = tasks were pushed to agents but not completed. Metrics exceeding interval duration occur because concurrent interactions are counted additively (agent handles 3 chats simultaneously → 3× the clock time). This is by design but counterintuitive. StatsDenormWorker backlog causes stale counts.

Resolution playbook

  1. Confirm which view the customer is quoting. Agents view = /supervision/users; Channels = /supervision/channels.
  2. Walk the customer through the distinction.
  3. For stuck interactions: impersonate agents, check workbins, manually complete or release.
  4. For metrics exceeding interval: explain the additive-time model for concurrent interactions. This is expected behavior, not a bug.
  5. For counts that never update: check StatsDenormWorker logs for the tenant in Kibana. If backlogged, escalate.

Typical customers

Orange, Frontier Airlines, The Hut.com, Asiacell.

Known architectural patterns worth knowing

The "Master Source" consolidation pattern

When a customer has many phone lines or mailboxes (one per team, one per location), they often create many content sources — one per line. This scales badly. The supported architecture is:

  • A single master source ingests everything.
  • The Rules Engine inspects the original dialed number / recipient address / forwarding "To" and applies a category.
  • The category routes the task to the correct channel group.
  • Category can be changed mid-thread to hand off between teams without duplicating the intervention.

Recommend this pattern for customers presenting with "source sprawl."

SCC (Structured Content Components)

Menu-button components surfaced in Messaging channels. Currently there is no first-class way to block typed replies when an SCC is active — the chat accepts free text even during an SCC interaction. Customers (Ziggo, SFR, RED) have asked for this and it has been discussed in EDEQ-518. There is no ETA.
